# Going Out to the Streets

>"If you want to design extraordinary experiences, you have to step out of the boardroom and into the world your customers live in—because true insight begins where your assumptions end.”

| Now You See Me |
| :---: |
|![](../../images/going_out_to_the_streets.png)|
|Observing the reality of your real customer|

>In the movie *Now You See Me*, the Four Horsemen captivate audiences with high-stakes illusions performed in real-world locations, not distant theaters. They stand face-to-face with the crowd, feed off their reactions, and challenge what people believe is possible — right there, in the street. Their success depends not on perfection behind the curtain, but on *intimacy with the audience*. It’s a powerful reminder for leaders and innovators: if you want to create magic for your customers, you need to *be where they are*, see what they see, and listen without filters.

In digital transformation, the guiding compass is not technology, methodology, or even a visionary business model—it is the relentless pursuit of a new, enchanting user experience. Without an organization’s deep, almost obsessive understanding of its value proposition through the lens of customer experience, no amount of innovation will take root. Technology alone will not save a product or service that fails to resonate with the reality of its users.

This pursuit demands that leaders, designers, product teams, marketers, and service staff leave their rooms, offices, and even the comfort of their own cultural biases to confront the reality of the customer. It means stepping away from dashboards, market reports, and internal brainstorming sessions and entering the actual environments—physical or digital—where customers live, work, and make decisions. This departure must be intentional and supported by a company culture that values field immersion as much as it does quarterly reports.

Observation in its purest form is the first step. Approach it like a detective—but not a secret one. The aim is to observe “in natura” without altering the customer’s behavior by making your presence known too soon. In the physical world, this means watching how they arrive, interact with the space, browse, touch, and evaluate products or services. Notice their expressions, hesitations, questions, and the alternative options they consider. In the digital realm, observation means reading the unfiltered truth: social media comments, customer reviews, complaint boards, session recordings, navigation paths, heatmaps, and bounce patterns. These are the traces of lived experience, captured in real time.

It is equally important to look beyond your core offering. What complementary services or tools do customers use to “complete” your value proposition? How do they discover and pay for your service? What friction points exist in their payment preferences, pricing perceptions, or frequency of use? Often, the core product addresses only a fraction of the customer’s full objective, leaving them to assemble their own ecosystem of adjacent solutions. If you don’t understand this ecosystem, you risk designing in isolation—and losing the customer entirely.

The scope of observation should therefore be comprehensive and expanded, aiming to understand the entire journey:
* Who your customers are, and their economic context.
* Why they seek your service and what specific problem or aspiration drives them.
* How they measure value—not just in price, but in perceived outcomes, convenience, and emotional satisfaction.
* What motivates them to stay, leave, or recommend.

Pricing and payment models are highly subjective and situational. Discounts for loyalty, referral bonuses, flexible payment terms, or even community-driven incentives can all shape the perceived value of your offering. Designing for the ultimate user experience means aligning these structural decisions with your observations.

Once the qualitative data is captured without judgement, you can shift into validation mode. Approach customers directly, explain your intent, and engage them in open-ended conversations. Let them narrate their own story: how the need arose, what they sought to accomplish, the outcomes they expect, and how they truly perceive the value received. This firsthand narrative enriches the observational data, grounding your insights in human truth rather than speculation.

From this process emerges the zero vector—a baseline of reality from which your digital transformation can be guided with precision. This is not just market research; it is empathy in action, the lived context that transforms a value proposition from an internal aspiration into a real-world experience that customers recognize, embrace, and champion.
