## Preface
>“Digital Transformation starts with you, not technology…”

The Digital Era has come to turn conventions upside down—where uncertainty takes the place of certainty, business cases give way to experimentation, and knowledge feuds yield to democratized information and open access at individual, community, and organizational levels. It is ubiquitous, and there is no turning back—only moving faster ahead, with the intent to transform society’s problems and aspirations into new norms of existence.

However, organizations still struggle with the effectiveness of their digital endeavors, and this often has more to do with the starting point of the journey than with their visions, aspirations, or technology procurement. Effective transformation comes from people—not from hiring a new Chief of Technology, Innovation, or Digital as a supposed savior for a weakened board of executives, nor from adopting agile processes, design methods, or digital technology alone. While these elements do have a role in the digital transformation journey, they are supporting players and tools—not the main driver that will create a truly innovative, adaptive, and transformative culture.

Digital Transformation is a journey unlike any other transformation program in human history—period. You may have past experience with transformation initiatives in your organization, but none have pushed every button at once—and discovered entirely new buttons you never knew existed, sometimes triggering desperation in people. Digital Transformation is drastically disruptive and demands new mindsets and leadership—some so counterintuitive and far from executive board consensus that it can feel like an impossible feat. “How can a caterpillar truly become a butterfly?” Well, we are humans, and by our very nature we have the ability to achieve the incredible, the miraculous, and the once-thought-impossible. This is the very essence that visionaries like Jeff Bezos, Elon Musk, Steve Jobs, Peter Diamandis, and others have been trying to convey—nudging people like you and me toward a new belief system.

If we do not pursue a human-centered, human-oriented approach to Digital Transformation at every level and across every area of an organization, the result will be fragmented—milestones achieved here and there, but not integrated, fully leveraged, or efficient enough to deliver the vision. This is not about a single “big bang” in terms of technical execution; it is a disruption in organizational structure, leadership approach, and thinking itself. The foundation is culture—where cultural traits determine success—and the cornerstone is digital leadership, keeping the culture engaged and evolving toward transformation.

Digital Transformation is not about adopting cloud computing while mobile application development and analytics happen in isolation somewhere else. It is about an integrated, human-centered, and human-oriented roadmap for both customers and employees—where your input may be your vision and business strategy, but the actual planning, execution, and delivery is carried out by empowered individuals obsessed with solving customer problems. It requires a willingness to move fast and fail fast, where learning is the compass guiding the way forward in the darkness of uncertainty. After all, no war is won without winning many battles along the way.

The take-up of digital accelerators remains too low despite their proven benefits. The question is no longer whether to “do digital,” but whether leaders have the concrete knowledge and skills to drive digital endeavors forward. According to reports, 83% of nonprofit leaders state that their biggest barrier is low digital maturity within their organizations, and 98% say they lack the resources to carry out digital initiatives. It’s not hard to see why—these leaders remain trapped in conventional thinking models: securing budgets, hiring people, retreating to offices, over-controlling non-core operations, and creating parallel, disconnected streams of projects that stretch the organization thin and lead to disengagement.

Digital Transformation cannot be driven from a purely top-down perspective, conceived in silos, or executed from ivory towers. Digital leaders must first align their objectives, learn deeply about the subject, breathe and live digital in their day-to-day lives, truly care about customers—including those they are not yet serving—and foster empathy among employees, customers, and the broader market. Only then can they create a unified, digitized mindset to ignite the journey. I don’t even call it a “program” or “project,” because such terms trap you into conventional thinking—planning for outcomes a year or two ahead as though the path is already clear. In truth, you will rarely see more than three months ahead, and only then can you determine the right step toward building a solid, consistent, healthy, and valued component of your digital capability model.

The World Economic Forum advises executives to focus on innovation and recognize the positive impact of new business models on society and the environment. Organizations face ever-increasing pressure to innovate continuously, with digital technologies playing a central role in these innovations. A rapidly expanding digital population is driving new consumption behaviors and collective phenomena, enabling new entrants—directly or indirectly—in virtually every industry and market. New economies are emerging, and selecting the right frameworks and conditions to enable sustainable digital endeavors that deliver continuous innovation has become a critical ingredient for organizational success.

Through many years of personal and direct involvement in digital initiatives across both business and technology, I have seen that the most valuable outcomes emerged in cases where we had the empowerment to make decisions, focused on real customers, established a clear model for multi-disciplinary collaboration, and operated from a vision that was compelling to everyone—meaningful to business results and rewarding for every team member. Leadership, therefore, is the crucial component in making digital transformation succeed in any organization.

After experimenting with countless practices, tools, methods, frameworks, and models, none proved as valuable as those driven by the factors above—because they transformed people, not just the things around them. Without that, everything eventually regresses to the old state of affairs, with more tasks on the shelf and more work yet to be done.

## Prologue
The contents of this book have been conceived, elaborated, and shaped over the past eight years. Back then—after a long career that began in 1993 working with digital (a term that didn’t even exist at the time)—I came to the realization that the human factor and leadership traits in digital-related projects and endeavors were the most decisive success factors, and the ones that yielded the greatest results. My mind simply clicked, generating many of the insights you will read in this book.

Today, the human factor is no longer a new topic. But years ago, it was not recognized as a foundational element for the digital age. This reality invites us to reflect and to gain new knowledge about people and society as never before. In a fast-paced digital era, where science produces astonishing discoveries and applications, society, businesses, and organizations across the globe face inconvenient truths that challenge the evolving mind. The human factor may no longer seem original—it is now widely acknowledged in the industry—but there remains a lack of thinking on new paradigms and motivations to truly lead the way. Of course, once you experience it personally, it changes everything. It becomes part of you—raising awareness and consciousness, blending personal and social experiences into a single continuum of human evolution.

The real issue in digital transformation lies in its complexity, variety, quality, and maturity—its vast content, practices, forums, methods, technical paraphernalia, and required skills. Yet we must not overlook the most common, and yet most neglected, human traits—the very qualities that make us human. These traits form the main pillar of sustainability, supporting the purpose, morality, and ethics of the entire journey we are undertaking. As human beings, we build solutions to address our own problems and those of our planet. We cannot operate as cold, emotionless machines, running controlled experiments with limited possible outcomes.
Of course, complexity is relative. For those who have mastered a subject, complexity diminishes drastically, and certain matters may appear simple—just as Einstein’s theory of relativity can be distilled to a core principle. But in digital transformation, the challenge is to piece everything together holistically. It is not something that can be truly understood from the outside—it must come from original, hands-on experiences in order to provide real value to communities seeking to thrive on continuous learning and positive human experiences.

I had to be careful not to oversimplify the content to the point of superficiality. My goal has been to pass on knowledge in a democratized and accessible way, supported by meaningful context, using storytelling and even playful elements to sustain attention, aid memorization, and ground ideas in practical situations. This is all in pursuit of the next levels of innovation and in overcoming known challenges within a structured and coherent body of knowledge. Most importantly, it is driven by a positive and optimistic purpose: now, more than ever, we must build profound empathy for ourselves and for others, and address the “wicked problems” humanity has tolerated for centuries. If digital transformation gives us a new level playing field, let us take the opportunity to build a fair, equal, and—why not—loving space in which to grow, build trust, and live in harmony within the bounds of reason and freedoms.

It all began with defining the knowledge domains—how broad, comprehensive, and relevant they are for leaders and all those engaged in digital development. From this, the Digital Transformation Canvas emerged. Over time, as my journey evolved and I faced new challenges, many other elements joined this collection of experiences, which I now call the Digital Hive. You can read this book in order, or you can select the “honeycomb” that interests you most among the eight domains and their foundational principles. Of course, it is highly recommended to go through its entirety, so different angles which form this hive become aware for you, and recognize how related they are to the entire framework. It's all about its summed parts, then parts on its own.

I have been fortunate to work on digital technology projects since I began my career at eighteen years old. It has been a remarkable learning journey. I’ve had the chance to experiment, implement, and innovate with cutting-edge technologies, practices, and methods—growing both professionally and personally. I have always embraced the fact that I am, in my own way, a singular conscious phenomenon. I consider myself lucky not only because I worked with advanced technologies, but also because I worked in entirely new forming departments and areas—uncharted terrain waiting to be built and explored. And ultimately, it always comes back to people—because they are the root of everything. Without them, my perspectives would be just a small, limited bubble of interpretations, decisions, and knowledge.

I am deeply thankful for the distinguished and diverse people I have met throughout my life—those who shared with me a passion for challenging the status quo and rejecting mediocrity. The warm and profound discussions we had, connected us on a deeper level and allowed me to learn and apply those lessons through experiments, projects, programs, and operations.

The book is structured into chapters, each with its own rationale. Within them are episodes classified by domain. Each episode includes its “branded theme,” a short inspirational and contextual quote, a rationale linking to the theme.

To close this prologue, I want to thank you for reading this far. I look forward to knowing you have read the book. Please feel free to engage with me in any way you wish—whether to discuss, challenge, or simply exchange experiences without any obligations.
